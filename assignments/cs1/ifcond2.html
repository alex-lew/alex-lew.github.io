1Caught speeding

You are driving a little too fast, and a police officer stops you. Write code to compute the result: “no ticket,” “small ticket,” or “big ticket.” If speed is 60 or less, the result is “no ticket”. If speed is between 61 and 80 inclusive, the result is “small ticket”. If speed is 81 or more, the result is “big ticket”. Unless it is your birthday–on that day, your speed can be 5 higher in all cases. The first argument is your speed, in MPH, and the second is whether or not it’s your birthday.

1
2
3
(check-expect (caught-speeding 60 #false) "no ticket")
(check-expect (caught-speeding 65 #false) "small ticket")
(check-expect (caught-speeding 65 #true) "no ticket")

Extra Challenge: Blackjack, Part 2

In the first Blackjack problem, you compared two players’ Blackjack scores. But how do we calculate these scores? In this problem, we assume a player’s hand has three cards, where each card is a number between 1 (Ace) and 13 (King). To get the score, you sum up the values of the cards: for example, if I have a 2, a 7, and a 10, then my score is 2 + 7 + 10 = 19. But there are two exceptions. First, anything above a 10 just counts as 10 points. Second, an ace (a 1) can count as 1 point OR 11 points–whichever is strategically best for the player. For example, if I have 10, 10, Ace (1), then my score is 21; I prefer to count the Ace as 1 point instead of 11 points, because 11 points would put me above 21. But if I have 5, 5, Ace (1), my score is also 21, not 11: I choose to count the Ace as 11 points.

Write a function blackjack-3 that takes in three card values between 1 and 13, and computes the total score.
